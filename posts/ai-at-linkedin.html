Learning to Build with AI: My First Few Months at LinkedIn

I came in as a data person. I didn't expect to become a builder. But somewhere between Cursor, Claude Code, and a Streamlit dashboard that refused to stop hanging, that's exactly what happened.

When I joined LinkedIn, I knew the role would be intellectually demanding. What I didn't fully anticipate was how different the work would feel from anything I'd done before. Not just in the problems themselves, but in the texture of the day-to-day. This is a post about that shift, and about the unexpected partner that made it possible.

A new kind of work

My role sits at the intersection of taxonomy design and LLM classification accuracy. In plain terms, I spend a lot of time thinking about how to teach an AI model to correctly understand what a customer is trying to say. That sounds narrow. In practice, it touches everything: how categories are defined, what language signals intent versus sentiment, and why two labels that sound similar can quietly confuse a model into picking the wrong one.

It's a domain where the craft is in the details. A single ambiguous sentence in a taxonomy description can silently tank accuracy across hundreds of conversations. Getting it right requires a mix of linguistic precision, statistical thinking, and a willingness to sit with confusion long enough for patterns to emerge.

What I didn't expect was how much building would be part of it.

Cursor + Claude Code changed something

I've always been comfortable in Python. I can wrangle data, build models, write pipelines. But I'm not a software engineer. Before these tools, there was a clear ceiling: I could analyze things, but building a real interface to share that analysis (something interactive, something polished) required either a front-end engineer or weeks of learning I didn't have time for.

Cursor Claude Code Streamlit Python Trino / SQL

Cursor changed the experience of writing code. Having an AI that understands the full context of a file, can suggest the next logical step, catch the bug you were about to introduce, or refactor older code without losing the intent, removes the friction that used to slow me down most. It doesn't make me a better programmer in the abstract. It makes the act of programming feel less like fighting syntax and more like thinking out loud.

Claude Code goes further. It's not just autocomplete; it's a collaborator that can hold the shape of a multi-step project, reason about tradeoffs, and execute changes across an entire codebase while explaining what it's doing. I've used it to debug queries, build out classification pipelines, refactor dashboard logic, and, yes, build this very website. Tasks that used to take most of a day now happen in a fraction of the time. Not because corners are being cut, but because the cognitive overhead of switching between thinking and typing has nearly disappeared.

"The question is no longer whether I can build something. It's whether I have a clear enough idea of what I want."

Streamlit: from analysis to product

One of the most tangible expressions of this shift has been building Streamlit dashboards. If you haven't used Streamlit, it's a Python library that lets you turn a script into a web app (charts, filters, tables, the works) without touching HTML or JavaScript. What used to require a full-stack team, I can now do myself in an afternoon.

But the real value isn't the speed. It's the closing of the loop. When you can take an analysis from notebook to shareable dashboard in the same session, stakeholders can interact with the data directly instead of waiting for your next slide deck. Questions get answered faster. The analysis stops being a deliverable and starts being a conversation.

I've built dashboards that give teams visibility into data they previously had to ask for. Each one taught me something about what people actually need to see versus what an analyst thinks they need to see. That gap is where most dashboards go wrong.

AI agents as actual partners

I want to be careful here, because this could easily tip into hype. So let me be specific.

AI agents are genuinely useful when the task has clear inputs, a defined goal, and enough complexity that doing it manually would be tedious or error-prone. Debugging a query. Iterating on descriptions based on a pattern of failures. Refactoring a codebase to follow a new convention. In these cases, delegating to an agent isn't about being lazy. It's about staying at the level of the problem rather than getting lost in the mechanics of the solution.

What surprised me most is how much better my own thinking got in the process. When you're working with an agent, you have to be precise about what you want. Vague instructions produce vague results. The discipline of specifying a goal clearly enough for an AI to act on it turns out to be the same discipline required to think clearly about a problem in the first place. It's a feedback loop that makes you sharper.

There are still things agents get wrong. They misread context. They confidently do the wrong thing. They sometimes solve a problem you didn't actually have. But so do humans. The difference is that with an agent, the cost of iterating is low. You can redirect, correct, and try again without the social friction of managing a person's feelings about their work.

What these first few months have taught me

I came into this role thinking my job was to understand data. That's still true. But I've learned that understanding data is only useful when it reaches the right person at the right time in the right form. Building is the bridge. And it turns out that bridge is now accessible to people like me: analytically minded, technically comfortable, but not engineers by training.

LinkedIn has been the right environment for this kind of growth. The problems are genuinely hard, and that pressure pushes you to reach for better tools and sharper thinking. It's the kind of place where figuring out a smarter way to work isn't optional.

The tools have made me faster. But more than that, they've made me more ambitious about what I think is possible to attempt. That might be the most lasting thing these past few months have given me.
